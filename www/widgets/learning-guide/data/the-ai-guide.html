CHAPTER 1

When Choosing to Use AI

Artificial "Intelligence" (AI) is surrounded by a lot of hype, and knowing when that hype is warranted requires an understanding of how it works. But understanding AI isn't a requirement for creatively coding online, what matters most is understanding the Web itself (You can click "back" to access all those notes). If you are curious about AI though, and how best to incorporate it into your creative practice, these notes are designed to help you make informed decisions about when, how, and whether to use this technology in your work.

CHAPTER 2
AI as Coding Assistants: Crafting the Right Prompt

It’s tempting, with coding assistants at hand, to “vibe-code” your way to something interesting. But your imagination is bounded by your understanding of the medium. If we turn to AI coding assistants too early and too often, our ideas become constrained by what a chatbot suggests, and while they're very profficient coders, they're not very creative thinkers.

That said, the future of coding will likely involve some degree of AI-assistance. To learn to use these systems in ways that benefit us, extending our capabilities and supporting our creativity rather than undermining our learning and limiting our ideas, we've created a widget to help you craft the most effective prompts.





CHAPTER 3
AI as Coding Assistants: Editor Integration with APIs

As explained in chapter 1, an app's graphical user interface (GUI) controls how you interact with the model behind it, limiting not only what you can do, but what you think to do, often in ways you might not realize. When we interact with an AI model directly, as code, we expand the creative possibilities beyond what the desginers of an app's GUI imagined for us. The first step we can take in that direction is to communicate directly with a model's "application programming interface" or API.

We've explained that netnet is a classical AI system, meaning everything it says was written by hand, nothing is synthesized by an LLM. However, you can connect netnet to an API having it act as a conduit or medium for your chosen LLM running in a datacenter.


This widget will reveal what it means for a writing/coding app to be "AI powered" by making that connection transparent. netnet will guide you through the process, allowing you to make some decisions (like choosing your preferred LLM provider and model settings) while making others for you (setting system prompt and response schema) to ensure we integrate LLMs in a way that serves our learning goals.





CHAPTER 4
AI as Creative Materials: Using Local Models in our Work

In chapter 3 we connected netnet to an LLM through a REST API, something you can also do in your own projects using models running in a datacenter or locally on your computer (as this demo explains). While popular LLMs are still large and resource-intensive, they're just one type of neural network.

Machine learning (ML) is about finding patterns in data to make predictions or generate output, and many smaller models with creative potential can now run directly in the browser. This comes with many advantages: they can be packaged with your project, require far less energy than datacenter models, and keep user data on-device to ensure privacy.


Transformers.js
One of the newest browser-based ML libraries is called Transformers.js by Hugging Face, it can be used to run pretrained transformer models (like LLMs and other NLP, vision and audio models). The demo below is a basic example using a sentiment analysis model, but keep in mind there are loads of other models you can use with this library.

1. Text Sentiment Analysis


TensorFlow.js
Another popular library for running and training ML models in the browser is TensorFlow.js by Google, it supports different neural network architectures. The examples below show creative uses of Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), one of the earliest and most widely used ML architectures. CNNs are typically trained to recognize patterns and predict "labels" from input data. In the following examples, they’re used to interpret images and audio in real time, detecting body and hand positions, as well as recognizing simple spoken commands, turning our cameras and mics into interactive controls.

3. Hand Tracking

Magenta.js
The following examples come from Magenta, a research team at Google that was among the first to explore how machine learning could be used creatively in art and music. These demos use earlier neural network architectures like Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs), Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), and Variational Autoencoders (VAEs). While much of today’s attention is on large transformer-based models, these older architectures are far from obsolete, they're lightweight and full of creative potential, especially for use directly in the browser.

CHAPTER 5
AI as Art: Crafting Neural Networks from Scratch

In earlier chapters we talked about how neural networks learn from data rather than following hand-written rules, but we kept things at a high level, using pre-trained models. In this chapter, we’ll look under the hood and walk through the process of creating a neural network from the ground up. The goal isn't to become machine learning engineers, but to develop a grounded understanding of the technology and to approach neural networks as creative material in their own right. While we won't be building cutting-edge models, you'll gain enough insight to experiment with and treat neural networks themselves as a form of artistic expression.
